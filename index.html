Cape Verde was not discovered. It was built.

There is a version of this story in which the islands are a footnote, a watering stop between continents that mattered more. We do not argue with that version. We go closer to the source and let depth win.

These ten volcanic islands held no permanent settlement when the first ships arrived in the 1460s. Then the peoples of the Atlantic, West African and Portuguese, enslaved and free, were forced together on dry rock. Out of that encounter they built what no single continent can claim: the oldest living creole language, a music the whole world borrows for its longing, a way of carrying home in the body. Mixture is not dilution here. Mixture is the achievement. Morabeza has no entry in any empire's dictionary.

These pages are for Cape Verdeans everywhere, and above all for the ones far from the islands: Rotterdam, Boston, Lisbon, Paris, Luxembourg. Many of us grew up feeling Cape Verdean without ever being handed the whole story. Here it is, told the way someone whose grandmother is buried there would tell it.

Every piece is checked against the record, and the sources are printed at the end of each one, where everyone can read them. Nu ta konta nôs stória, djuntu: we tell our story together.

Cape Verde and the slave trade: the honest story

Ribeira Grande was the Atlantic's first slave entrepôt. We read the ledger with our own names in both columns.

Before Cape Verde there was a rehearsal. When Portuguese ships pushed out into the Atlantic in the early fourteen hundreds they found, one after another, the island chains geographers now call Macaronesia, and the first finds were paradise. Madeira was green to the waterline, timbered, watered, empty; the Azores rose out of the sea mist like pasture that had never known a fence. Within a generation Madeira was terraced, burning and planted in sugarcane, and a settlement recipe emerged: find the bay, find the stream, build the chapel, divide the land among captains, and make the island feed the mainland's ambitions.

Some forty years after Madeira, the ships found the final and driest archipelago of the set. There was nothing to conquer and no one to convert, only dry volcanic rock in a supremely useful position, on the sea road to everywhere. Continue reading →
