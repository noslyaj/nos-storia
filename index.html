Santo Antão · São Vicente · Santa Luzia · São Nicolau · Sal · Boa Vista · Maio · Santiago · Fogo · Brava panu di terra: ten gold threads, ten islands

Cape Verde was not discovered. It was built.

There is a version of this story in which the islands are a footnote, a watering stop between continents that mattered more. We do not argue with that version. We go closer to the source and let depth win.

No one had ever lived on these ten volcanic islands when the first ships arrived in the 1460s. Then the peoples of the Atlantic, West African and Portuguese, enslaved and free, were forced together on dry rock. Out of that encounter they built what no single continent can claim: the oldest living creole language, a music the whole world borrows for its longing, a way of carrying home in the body. Mixture is not dilution here. Mixture is the achievement. Morabeza has no entry in any empire's dictionary.

These pages are for Cape Verdeans everywhere, and above all for the ones far from the islands: Rotterdam, Boston, Lisbon, Paris, Luxembourg. Many of us grew up feeling Cape Verdean without ever being handed the whole story. Here it is, told the way someone whose grandmother is buried there would tell it.

Every piece is checked against the record, and the sources are printed at the end of each one, where everyone can read them. Nu ta konta nôs stória, djuntu: we tell our story together.

Amílcar Cabral: the revolutionary they couldn't colonize

He counted the empire's fields before he took them back.

In 1953 a young engineer walked into the interior of Portuguese Guinea with notebooks, measuring tapes and a government commission. Lisbon wanted an agricultural census. The engineer went village to village, hundreds upon hundreds of them, recording groundnut yields, rice paddies, soil types, the weight of what people grew and the weight of what was taken from them. The colonial administration read the finished census as data. The engineer had read it, while walking it, as a map of everything wrong.

His name was Amílcar Cabral. Within twenty years, the empire that commissioned his survey would lose the territory he surveyed, in no small part because of what he learned by walking it. Continue reading →
