Amílcar Cabral: the revolutionary they couldn't colonize

He counted the empire's fields before he took them back.

In 1953 a young engineer walked into the interior of Portuguese Guinea with notebooks, measuring tapes and a government commission. Lisbon wanted an agricultural census. The engineer went village to village, hundreds upon hundreds of them [VERIFY: sources commonly state he surveyed most of the territory; confirm the figure before citing a number], recording groundnut yields, rice paddies, soil types, the weight of what people grew and the weight of what was taken from them. The colonial administration read the finished census as data. The engineer had read it, while walking it, as a map of everything wrong.

His name was Amílcar Cabral. Within twenty years, the empire that commissioned his survey would lose the territory he surveyed, in no small part because of what he learned by walking it.

Cabral was a child of the crossing itself. He was born in Bafatá, in Portuguese Guinea, on 12 September 1924, to Cape Verdean parents; his father Juvenal, a schoolteacher, came from Santiago. The family returned to Cape Verde when Amílcar was a boy [VERIFY: year of return, commonly given as 1932], and he grew up between Santiago and São Vicente, finishing at the liceu in Mindelo as one of the most brilliant students the islands had produced. He also grew up inside catastrophe. The droughts of the 1940s starved Cape Verde twice over: the famines of 1941 to 1943 and 1947 to 1948 killed tens of thousands of people out of a population that had little more than a hundred thousand to lose [VERIFY: mortality figures; estimates in the literature run to some 45,000 across the two famines; confirm against Carreira]. The empire counted the dead and shipped no rescue worth the name. A teenager with Cabral's eyes did not forget the arithmetic. He chose agronomy, he said later, because of what the drought had shown him: if the land could be made to hold, the people could be made to stay alive.

A scholarship carried him to Lisbon, to the Instituto Superior de Agronomia, where he finished near the top of his class in a school full of the empire's sons. The education cut both ways, as colonial educations do. In the Casa dos Estudantes do Império, the residence Lisbon kept for its promising colonials, Cabral fell in with Agostinho Neto of Angola, Mário Pinto de Andrade and the small circle that would later set half an empire alight. They read what they were not supposed to read and called their project the re-Africanization of the spirit: the slow work of unlearning the colonizer inside one's own head. It is a very Cape Verdean beginning. First the language of the master, mastered. Then the turn.

Posted back to Guinea in 1952 as an agronomist, Cabral took the census assignment no one else wanted and turned it into the deepest reconnaissance any African liberation movement ever conducted. A man who studies soil erosion learns how empires work: what holds, what washes away, and how long the washing takes. He learned which peoples grew rice and which grew groundnuts, who resented the tax and who resented the chief the tax came through, where the grievances pooled like water after rain. In September 1956, in Bissau, he and a handful of comrades, Aristides Pereira and his own brother Luís among them, founded the PAIGC: the African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde. The name was the program. Two territories, one struggle, stitched together in the acronym the way his own family was stitched across the water. Portugal called its colonies eternal. Cabral had measured their soil and knew better.

The lesson that set the course came in blood. On 3 August 1959, dockworkers at the Pidjiguiti quay in Bissau struck for better pay, and colonial police and armed civilians fired into them, killing dozens [VERIFY: death toll; accounts range from about 25 to over 50]. Cabral drew the conclusion without theatrics: the cities belonged to the empire's guns, so the struggle would move to the countryside, and it would move slowly, politically, his way. Cadres trained across the border in Conakry. Years of patient village work came before the first shot of the armed struggle in January 1963. He drilled one discipline into his militants above all the others, in words that traveled far beyond his war: tell no lies, claim no easy victories.

What the PAIGC built in its liberated zones is the part the textbooks skim, and it is the part that matters most here. People's stores replaced the colonial trading posts. Bush schools taught children to read while the war went on over their heads; clinics treated villagers who had never seen a nurse; village committees, with women required among their members, settled disputes the Portuguese courts had never bothered to hear. Cabral insisted the fight was not against the Portuguese people, and not even, in the deepest sense, for a flag: national liberation, he argued in lectures now studied from Havana to Syracuse, is an act of culture. A colonized people frees itself by returning to the source, taking back its own history as the raw material of its future. By 1972 the party governed most of the countryside in fact, and a United Nations mission traveled through the liberated zones that April to see a state functioning inside a colony that officially did not permit one.

For Cape Verde itself the strategy was different, and the difference shows how precisely he thought. No guerrilla war was ever fought on the islands. Ten small, dry, watchable islands offer a rebel nowhere to stand, and Cabral, who had grown up on them, knew it better than the empire did. Cape Verdeans instead became the struggle's nervous system, staffing the PAIGC's leadership, its diplomacy and its schools in exile out of all proportion to their numbers, and the plan was always that the islands would be carried to freedom on the mainland's war. It worked. When the empire finally broke, Cape Verde walked to its independence through negotiation and the ballot box, in a vote the PAIGC won overwhelmingly in June 1975 [VERIFY: characterize the June 1975 transitional elections precisely], without a single battle scarring the archipelago. The driest islands in the Atlantic were liberated by an argument.

He did not live to see the flags. On the night of 20 January 1973, outside his home in Conakry, Amílcar Cabral was shot dead by men from inside his own movement. How far the Portuguese secret police reached into the plot is still argued in the archives; that the plot failed in its larger purpose is not argued at all. The party held. In September 1973 the PAIGC declared the independence of Guinea-Bissau, and dozens of states recognized it within weeks. In April 1974 the Carnation Revolution brought the regime down in Lisbon, worn through in no small part by the African wars it could not win. Guinea-Bissau's independence was recognized that September. And on 5 July 1975, Cape Verde raised its own flag. His brother Luís led the new Guinea-Bissau. His comrade Aristides Pereira became the first president of Cape Verde. The agronomist's harvest came in on time.

It matters where a revolutionary is made. Cabral was made in the crossing: Guinean earth, Cape Verdean blood, a Lisbon education he turned inside out. The empire's quiet assumption was that mixture produced people who belonged to nobody. Cabral proved it produced people who could belong to everyone and answer to no one. In the liberated zones the fields were worked in common, djunta mon, hands joined, the old Cape Verdean way of surviving a dry year turned into a way of winning a war. Other revolutionaries of his century carried rifles into legend. His weapon of choice fit in a notebook, and it never missed.
