Cesária Évora: the barefoot diva who made sodade global

She set Mindelo's ache down uncut on the world's stages, and the world learned our word for it.

Halfway through a concert, in Paris or Tokyo or New York, the band would keep playing and Cesária Évora would sit down. A small table waited for her at the edge of the stage with a cigarette, an ashtray, a glass. She would smoke, unhurried, in front of two thousand people who had paid to watch her, and the room would hold its breath as if this too were part of the music. In a way it was. A woman from Mindelo does not perform her rest. She takes it.

She was born in that port city on 27 August 1941, on São Vicente, into a Mindelo past its boom: a coaling and cable station the steamships were already forgetting, a town rich in music and short of everything else. Her father, a musician, died when she was seven. Her mother cooked for other families to keep her own fed, and Cesária spent part of her girlhood in an orphanage [VERIFY: duration and details of the orphanage years]. By her mid-teens she was singing in the bars along the harbor, mornas and coladeiras for sailors and stevedores, and by her twenties the town had crowned her informally: the queen of Mindelo's nights, paid mostly in drinks and renown. Among the songs she carried were those of the great composer B.Léza, whom Mindelo counted as family to her [VERIFY: exact kinship between Cesária Évora and B.Léza], and by the 1960s her voice was going out over the local radio [VERIFY: which station and years].

It helps to know what a morna is, because she never sang anything that mattered more. A morna is a slow song in the minor key of a port town: guitar, cavaquinho, a violin if the night can afford one, and a voice keeping time with something slower than the band. It is music for staying up after the ship has gone. Mindelo did not invent longing, but it perfected the form that holds it, and by twenty Cesária owned that form the way a stevedore owns his hook.

Then, for most of the 1970s, she stopped. The story is told different ways, and she told it different ways herself: no money in it, no future in it, a newly independent country with more urgent debts than music, a singer who was tired in a way sleep does not fix. A voice like hers went quiet in the bars where it had been furniture, and the town let it. Ten years is a long intermission. Remember it whenever the legend is told as if it were an ascent with no floor.

The return began quietly, with recording trips to Lisbon in the mid-1980s arranged by fellow Cape Verdean musicians [VERIFY: the role of Bana and others in the Lisbon sessions]. In Lisbon she was heard by José da Silva, a young Frenchman of Cape Verdean parents [VERIFY: da Silva's occupation at the time, often given as a railway worker], who staked what he had on a middle-aged singer nobody outside the islands knew, and founded the Lusafrica label largely to carry her. Paris sessions followed. La Diva aux Pieds Nus came in 1988 and named her; Mar Azul followed in 1991. Then, in 1992, Miss Perfumado, and in its grooves an old morna of disputed parentage called Sodade, a fight over authorship we will tell in its own piece. The record sold in the hundreds of thousands and did for a Kriolu word what fado had once done for a Portuguese one. She was fifty-one years old when the world discovered what São Vicente had known for thirty-five.

The word deserves a paragraph of its own, because it is the true export. Sodade is kin to the Portuguese saudade, but it is not a borrowed word so much as a lived one, remade by a people whose entire history is departure: contract ships to São Tomé, packet boats to New Bedford, planes to Rotterdam and Lisbon and Luxembourg. It names the ache of an ocean between you and what is yours, and the certainty that it is still yours. When she sang it, emigrants wept in their seats in languages she did not speak. Nothing needed translating. That was the point of her, and the proof of us.

The fame, when it came, was enormous and she wore it like a housecoat. A Grammy in 2004 for Voz d'Amor, in the world music category the industry keeps for what it cannot file. Duets with singers from Brazil, Cuba, France. Millions of records sold across her late career [VERIFY: cumulative sales figure before citing one]. And through all of it, the bare feet on every stage in the world. Asked why, she gave different answers on different nights, solidarity with the barefoot poor of her islands among them, and she never once dressed the answer up into a slogan. The point stood without explanation, the way she preferred everything to stand. At home in Mindelo, between tours, her door stayed open and her table stayed full, and half the city could tell you what she cooked.

Her concerts doubled as national territory. In Paris and Rotterdam and Boston the halls filled with Cape Verdeans two and three generations deep: grandmothers who had packed panos in their suitcases decades before, grandchildren who knew the islands mainly as a dialing code and a smell in their aunt's kitchen. For three hours a rented room became the eleventh island. She sang Petit Pays and Sangue de Beirona, the room answered with the lines it knew, and strangers who could not have placed each other's home islands on a map went out into the cold as cousins. Statehood is one kind of nationhood. A tour schedule, it turns out, is another.

What she opened did not close behind her. Before Miss Perfumado, Cape Verdean music lived in community halls and cassette stalls from Dakar to Brockton; after it, the islands became a catalogue the world went looking for, and a generation of singers and players walked out through the door she had shouldered open. In time the country would count its music among its exports with a straight face. None of this was her stated ambition. She wanted, by her own account, a house, her family fed, and her cigarettes, and she kept saying so to interviewers who were waiting for a grander answer. The grandeur was structural. One woman's refusal to translate had repriced an entire culture.

Her heart began failing her in her last years, with surgeries and strokes from 2008 on, and she stopped touring in 2011 with a sentence of plain fact: she had no strength left. She died in Mindelo on 17 December 2011, seventy years old, a few miles from the bars where she had started. São Vicente's airport now carries her name, and her face is on the two thousand escudo note. A barefoot woman on the money of her country: somewhere an old colonial accountant is still trying to make that line balance.

Every small culture is told the same lie, that it must translate itself in order to travel. Cesária never translated. She took the most local thing we own, an ache with a Kriolu name, and set it down uncut in the biggest rooms on earth, and the rooms came to her. That is the entire argument of these pages, carried in one voice. The mixture is the achievement. The ache is the proof of belonging. And belonging, sung properly, needs no subtitles.
