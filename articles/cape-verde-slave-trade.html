Cape Verde and the slave trade: the honest story

Ribeira Grande was the Atlantic's first slave entrepôt. We read the ledger with our own names in both columns.

In the main square of Cidade Velha, on the island of Santiago, stands a marble column about the height of two men. Tourists photograph it because it is old and the light is good. It is a pelourinho, a pillory, raised in the early sixteenth century [VERIFY: commonly dated to around 1512 to the 1520s; confirm], and human beings were chained to it and whipped at it, in public, for the instruction of other human beings who were made to watch. It has stood in that square for five hundred years. This is where our story starts, because this is where it started.

The islands held no permanent settlement when the Portuguese came. Whatever mainland sailors may once have known of these shores, no one had ever built a village on them; there was nothing to conquer and no one to convert, only dry volcanic rock in a supremely useful position, three hundred miles off the Senegambian coast, on the sea road to everywhere. Santiago was settled in 1462. In 1466 the Crown granted the settlers of Ribeira Grande a charter to trade along the Rivers of Guinea, and read plainly, past its legal Latin, the charter was a license to deal in human beings. The town that grew from it became the first European colonial city in the tropics, and it grew rich in the only trade it had.

The mechanics deserve to be stated without decoration, because the decoration is where the lying usually happens. Ships out of the Upper Guinea coast, from Cacheu and the rivers, brought captive men, women and children to Santiago. Some were shipped onward quickly. Others were held on the island, put to work, baptized, renamed in Portuguese, taught enough of the language to obey, and then resold at a premium under the word the traders used for them: ladinos, the seasoned. Cape Verde's first industry was the manufacture of that word. The font where the renaming was done still exists; the church of Nossa Senhora do Rosário, built in 1495, is the oldest colonial church in the tropics, and its records are a registry of erasures performed one soul at a time. By 1533 Ribeira Grande held a bishop's seat, the first diocese of West Africa, raised on the labor of the people it baptized.

The islands did not only receive the trade. They staffed it, and, stranger than that, they seeded something inside it. From Santiago went the lançados, literally the thrown ones: traders, many of them exiles and New Christians keeping ahead of the Inquisition, who threw themselves ashore on the Guinea rivers against royal law, married into African trading families, learned the languages of the coast, and raised children who belonged to both banks of the water. The Crown outlawed them repeatedly and could never do without them. In their river settlements historians now recognize the first Luso-African creole communities, the mainland cousins of what the islands themselves were becoming. The line between colonizer and colonized, so clean in the imperial ledgers, blurred within a generation of the charter. Nothing about our beginnings was simple, and we decline to simplify them now.

Here the story turns a blade on itself, and we do not look away. Among the enslaved were weavers from the mainland, and on Santiago and Fogo they wove what the islands still call panos: narrow strips of indigo and white cotton, patterned on the loom and sewn side by side into cloth. The cloth was fine, and everyone on both sides of the water wanted it, so the traders made it money. Panos became a recognized currency of the coastal trade, units of account in the very commerce that had carried their weavers to the islands in chains [VERIFY: the barafula as trade currency, denominations and dates, per Carreira and Green]. The same cloth wraps Cape Verdean waists at every batuku today, and this page carries its pattern at the head of our front page. We wear it knowing both things at once. That is what honesty costs, and what it is worth.

The ledgers survive, and they point west. In the registers of the early Spanish American trade, Cape Verde appears again and again as a point of departure. One study of registered voyages to Cartagena de Indias, the great slave port of the Spanish Main, counts roughly nine thousand of some eleven thousand registered captives in its sample passing through Cape Verde [VERIFY: the figure of ~9,000 of 11,196 comes from the project brief; confirm the study, period and exact numbers against Newson and Minchin 2007 and Wheat 2016 before publication]. Behind every entry in those registers was a person with a name the register did not record. We state the numbers because the numbers are owed; we do not perform them. They are the floor of the truth, not its ceiling.

Numbers on the island itself tell the rest. Within a century of settlement, the enslaved were the great majority of Santiago's population [VERIFY: contemporary estimates put the enslaved share far above half by the late 1500s; confirm figure and source]. The free minority lived off their labor and their sale, raising cotton and horses for the coastal trade, indigo for the dye pits, cattle for hides, nearly every product either an input to the traffic or a sideline of it. And drought policed the whole arrangement. When the rains failed, those who could shipped out what they owned, and the enslaved and the newly freed starved first: a hierarchy of hunger the islands would keep long after the trade itself was gone.

Wealth on an open coast invites its own end. Francis Drake sacked Ribeira Grande in 1585. The French corsair Jacques Cassard finished the work in 1712, and the capital eventually moved down the coast to Praia. The trade routes straightened, running direct from the African coast to the Americas without the island stopover, and the entrepôt fell quiet. The empire moved its attention elsewhere, as empires do. The people could not move theirs. They were home now, whether home had chosen them or not.

Because all along, in the dry interior of Santiago, far from the ports and the pillory, another society had been assembling itself. Forros, the freed. Badius, the ones who ran to the hills and the ones who simply outlasted, planting maize in the ribeiras, keeping drum rhythms the church kept banning, marrying and burying and raising children in a language no ship had brought. Kriolu was born in the holding pens and the great houses and the hills between them, out of a Portuguese word-stock, a West African substrate, and sheer necessity. The first thing built in Cape Verde was a market in human beings. The second was a people. The people are still here. The market is a museum.

Some nations descend from the ships' holds and some from the ships' decks. We descend from both, and the pelourinho in the square indicts our ancestors twice over, as the whipped and as the watching. Cidade Velha was inscribed as a World Heritage site in 2009, and the column still stands, because taking it down would be the softer lie. We are not the heirs of a paradise. We are the heirs of a crime scene where, against every instruction, a civilization decided to be born. Built, not discovered, was never a boast. It is a confession and a triumph in the same breath.
