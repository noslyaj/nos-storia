Cabo Verde and the slave trade: the honest story

Ribeira Grande was the Atlantic's first slave entrepôt. We read the ledger with our own names in both columns.

Before Cabo Verde there was a rehearsal. When Portuguese ships pushed out into the Atlantic in the early fourteen hundreds they found, one after another, the island chains geographers now call Macaronesia, and the first finds were paradise. Madeira was green to the waterline, timbered, watered, empty; the Azores rose out of the sea mist like pasture that had never known a fence. The Crown's men barely paused to marvel. Within a generation Madeira was terraced, burning and planted in sugarcane, its forests felled for the boiling houses, its fields worked in part by enslaved hands, and its sugar making Lisbon rich. A settlement recipe emerged, repeated so faithfully that the settlers even carried their place names with them: find the bay, find the stream, build the chapel, divide the land among captains, and make the island feed the mainland's ambitions. There is a Ribeira Grande in the Azores to this day. It would not be the last.

Some forty years after Madeira, the ships found the final and driest archipelago of the set. Whoever sighted the islands first, and three men would spend their lives quarreling over the credit, what they saw was the opposite of Madeira: bare volcanic slopes, salt flats, valleys that promised water only in a good year. An island tradition, set down in print by the 1840s and weighed by the historian António Carreira, holds that Jalof seafarers from the mainland had known Santiago before the caravels; no trace of any visit has ever been found in the ground. Beyond that, the islands held no permanent settlement when the Portuguese came. Whatever mainland sailors may once have known of these shores, no one had ever built a village on them; there was nothing to conquer and no one to convert, only dry volcanic rock in a supremely useful position, three hundred miles off the Senegambian coast, on the sea road to everywhere.

Santiago was settled in 1462, and the recipe was unpacked on schedule: the bay, the stream, the chapel, the captains, and a town once again named Ribeira Grande. The settlers tried the Madeira playbook first. Sugarcane went into the wettest valleys as the opening experiment, new crops were trialed for export, and the drylands were given over to cotton and to horses, which the Guinea coast prized. But rain decides what an island can be, and these islands had little. What Santiago had instead was position. In 1466 the Crown granted the settlers of Ribeira Grande a charter to trade along the Rivers of Guinea, and read plainly, past its legal Latin, the charter was a license to deal in human beings. The town became the first European colonial city in the tropics, and it grew rich in the only trade its climate had left it.

The mechanics deserve to be stated without decoration, because decoration is where the lying usually happens. Upper Guinea was not one country. It was a crowded coast of rival lineages, faiths and war chiefs, Wolof and Mandinka, Biafada and Pepel and many more, taxing and raiding and warring as neighbors do. The newcomers did not conquer that world; they inserted themselves into its quarrels, arriving with horses, iron, cloth and wine, and leaving with the captives those quarrels produced. Ships out of Cacheu and the rivers brought captive men, women and children to Santiago, and the island learned the clerical work of the trade.

Say who, and say from where, because the empire's word Guinea was built to blur exactly that. In the earliest surviving sales records from the first decades of the trade, the great majority of captives sold out of Cabo Verde are labeled Jalofo: people of the Jolof empire and its provinces, Cayor, Baol, Waalo, in the northwest of today's Senegal, where Portuguese and island traders had exchanged horses for human beings since the 1440s. With them came Sereer from the small kingdoms of Siin and Saloum, and Mandinka from the Gambia river and the empire of Kaabu, which reached across today's Gambia, the Casamance and eastern Guinea-Bissau. As the trade slid south into the rivers, the registers fill with the peoples of what is now Guinea-Bissau and southern Senegal: Papel and Manjak from around Cacheu and Bissau, Banhun from the Cacheu river, Biafada from the Geba and the Rio Grande, Balanta from the flatlands between them, Felupe from the Casamance, Bijagó from the archipelago offshore, Fula from the inland plateaus. One caution the documents themselves teach: Jalofo in a ledger often named the empire a person was taken from, not the tongue their mother spoke. Even so, these are the nations Cabo Verde is made of. They have names, and the names have places, and both belong in print.

Most of the captives were dispatched onward with little delay, and in this early era the arrows on the map point not to Brazil, whose great forced crossings came later and sailed direct, but to the Spanish Indies: to Hispaniola, the island that is today the Dominican Republic, and above all toward Cartagena, on the coast of present-day Colombia. Others were kept on Santiago, sometimes for years, and put through a forced assimilation with a price attached: baptized, renamed in Portuguese, set to work in the cotton fields and the cane, taught enough of the language to obey. These were resold at a premium under the word the traders used for them: ladinos, the seasoned. Cabo Verde's first industry was the manufacture of that word. And its echo carries further than most people know: a line of linguists, most thoroughly Bart Jacobs, argues that Papiamentu, the creole spoken on Curaçao and Aruba to this day, descends from the Creole of Upper Guinea, carried west in the seventeenth century. The descent is still debated among scholars; the family resemblance is not. It is a thread this archive will follow in a piece of its own. The font where the renaming was done still exists; the church of Nossa Senhora do Rosário, built in 1495, is the oldest colonial church in the tropics, and its registers are an accounting of erasures performed one soul at a time. By 1533 Ribeira Grande held a bishop's seat, the first diocese of West Africa, raised on the labor of the people it baptized.

The islands did not only receive the trade. They staffed it, and, less known, they seeded something inside it. From Santiago went the lançados, literally the thrown ones: traders, many of them exiles, Jews and New Christians keeping ahead of the Inquisition, who threw themselves ashore on the Guinea rivers against royal law, married into African trading families, learned the languages of the coast, and raised children who belonged to both banks of the water. The Crown outlawed them repeatedly and could never do without them. And they carried the islands' newest invention along: for generations a Creole of Cape Verdean stamp served as the lingua franca of the river trade, so that the coast's business, including its darkest business, was conducted in the accents of Santiago. In their settlements historians recognize the first Luso-African creole communities, and the line between colonizer and colonized, so clean in the imperial ledgers, blurred within a generation of the charter. Nothing about our beginnings was simple, and we decline to simplify them now.

Among the enslaved were weavers from the mainland, and on Santiago and Fogo they wove, early on, what the islands still call panu di terra: narrow strips of indigo and white cotton, patterned on the loom and sewn side by side into cloth. The cloth was fine, and everyone on both sides of the water wanted it, so the traders made it money. Island owners sought out weavers and spinners by nation, Mandinka, Banhun, Sereer and Wolof above all; the Cape Verdean trader Lemos Coelho noted in 1684 that Jalofo women skilled at spinning were prized precisely for that skill. Panos became a recognized unit of account of the coastal trade: in surviving seventeenth century trading ledgers, a human being could be entered at a value of one hundred and fifty panos. The cloth that had carried its weavers to the islands in chains now priced the people who came after them. The same cloth wraps Cape Verdean waists at every batuku today, and its pattern runs across the head of our front page. We wear it knowing both things at once. That is what honesty costs, and what it is worth.

The ledgers survive, and they point west. In the paperwork of the early Spanish American trade, Cabo Verde appears again and again as the point of acquisition. In the registers kept at Seville, in the one decade so far counted in full, 1569 to 1579, some 11,196 captives were registered for shipment to the Spanish Americas, and nearly 9,000 of them were to be acquired in Cabo Verde. Behind every entry in those registers was a person with a name the register did not record. We state the numbers because the numbers are owed; we do not perform them. They are the floor of the truth, not its ceiling.

Numbers on the islands themselves tell the rest. In 1582 the sargento-mor Francisco de Andrade reported to the Crown that Santiago and Fogo together held some 13,700 enslaved people against roughly 2,000 free. The free minority lived off their labor and their sale, raising cotton and horses for the coastal trade, indigo for the dye pits, cattle for hides, nearly every product either an input to the traffic or a sideline of it. And drought policed the whole arrangement. When the rains failed, those who could shipped out what they owned, and the enslaved and the newly freed starved first: a hierarchy of hunger the islands would keep long after the trade itself was gone.

Wealth on an open coast invites its own end. Francis Drake sacked Ribeira Grande in 1585. The French corsair Jacques Cassard finished the work in 1712, and the capital eventually moved down the coast to Praia. The trade routes straightened, running direct from the African coast to the Americas without the island stopover, and the entrepôt fell quiet. The empire moved its attention elsewhere, as empires do. The people could not move theirs. They were home now, whether home had chosen them or not.

Because all along, in the dry interior of Santiago, far from the ports, another society had been assembling itself. Forros, the freed. Badius, the ones who ran to the hills and the ones who simply outlasted, planting maize in the ribeiras, keeping drum rhythms the church kept banning, marrying and burying and raising children in a language no ship had brought. Kriolu was born in the holding pens and the great houses and the hills between them, out of a Portuguese word-stock, a West African substrate, and sheer necessity. The first thing built in Cabo Verde was a market in human beings. The second was a people. The people are still here. The market is a museum.

And then the arithmetic flipped, in a way almost no other Atlantic slave society can claim. Manumission on the islands was common, and in the famine years it was even convenient: owners freed people they could no longer feed, a mercy with the accounting of an abandonment. Runaways stayed run. Freedom bred freedom. By the census of 1731, more than four out of five Cape Verdeans were free, while slavery was still fully legal, and the free did the work empires claim for themselves: it was Cape Verdeans out of Santiago and Fogo who peopled the rest of their own archipelago. Santo Antão and São Nicolau filled through the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries; Brava took in families fleeing Fogo's volcano after the eruption of 1680; and when Mindelo was founded on São Vicente in 1795, the settlers who would make it a city came overwhelmingly from the islands next door, not from Portugal. Slavery itself died slowly and on paper, its people reclassified as libertos in 1869 and freed outright only in the late 1870s. But the country that abolition decree arrived in had long since been built, populated and named by the formerly enslaved and their children. The empire settled two islands in earnest. Cape Verdeans settled the rest.

None of it was ever secure, because the climate had a vote and Lisbon mostly abstained. Drought is the archipelago's oldest resident, and when the rains failed for a second or third year the islands starved, again and again from the 1770s to the 1940s, while the metropole answered with paperwork, late ships, and more than once with nothing at all. The status the empire assigned us made the neglect easier to file. Cape Verdeans were classed as assimilated, citizens on paper, spared the indigenato codes that branded the peoples of Angola, Mozambique and Guinea as natives; and yet the islands were never Madeira or the Azores, never simply Portugal offshore. They were a colony, governed for extraction, taxed and priced like one. Portuguese when it flattered Lisbon, African when it cost money. So the people learned to save themselves, and mostly they did it by sea. From the eighteenth century, American whaling ships out of Massachusetts and Rhode Island took on crews at Brava and Fogo, the first thread of what became New England. In the worst years, contract ships carried the desperate south to the cocoa fields of São Tomé, a bargain cruel enough to need its own telling. Every route out began from the same conclusion: no one was coming.

Some nations descend from the ships' holds and some from the ships' decks. We descend from both. In the main square of Cidade Velha a marble pelourinho still stands, the pillory where human beings were whipped in public for the instruction of other human beings made to watch, and it indicts our ancestors twice over, as the whipped and as the watching. Cidade Velha was inscribed as a World Heritage site in 2009, and the column keeps its place in the square, because taking it down would be the softer lie. We are not the heirs of a paradise. We are the heirs of a crime scene where, against every instruction, a civilization decided to be born. Built, not discovered, was never a boast. It is a confession and a triumph in the same breath.
